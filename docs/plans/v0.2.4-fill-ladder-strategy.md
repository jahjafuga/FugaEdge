# v0.2.4 — Fill Ladder: In-Depth Rebuild Strategy

**Status:** Strategy / planning. No code yet.
**Base commit:** `449e76c` (fullscreen + screenshot export + blob-dot fills + Avg Entry/Exit lines — verified working, freeze-free).
**Prior attempt:** archived on branch `wip/v0.2.4-freeze-setaside` (`74531da`). Set aside, not deleted — its pure layout math is reusable.
**Author of record:** Lao (planning/oversight via Claude; build via Claude Code).

---

## 1. What we are building, in one sentence

For every fill in a trade, show a **dot at the exact fill price on its bar**, a **thin leader line**, and a **pill** reading `QTY @ PRICE` (green = buy, red = sell), placed in clear space and fanned apart when fills cluster — all on top of the clean chart we already have, without ever re-introducing the freeze.

This is the "pinpoint entry information" momentum day traders love: not just *that* they were in the trade, but exactly **how many shares at exactly what price**, drawn precisely where it happened.

---

## 2. Why this matters (the product case)

Momentum day traders (Ross Cameron style — the DTSM cohort) scale in and out of positions in multiple fills. A single trade might be five buys laddered up and three sells laddered out. The *story* of the trade is in those fills:

- Did they add into strength or chase?
- Where exactly did they start trimming?
- How big was each clip relative to the move?

The Avg Entry / Avg Exit lines (already shipped) tell the *summary*. The fill ladder tells the *execution story* — and that's the part a trader replays after the session to learn. This is a differentiator: most journals show average entry only. FugaEdge would show the exact shape of the scale-in/scale-out, annotated.

---

## 3. What the target actually specifies (read from the two SVGs + two screenshots)

### 3a. The normal case (`ladder_fill_markers_trade61.svg`, and the dots already in Screenshot 2)
Each fill is composed of three elements:
1. **Dot** — small filled circle (r≈4) at `(bar_x, fill_price_y)`, green `#3fb389` for buys, red `#e06b6b` for sells, with a **dark stroke** (`#0c0f16`, 1.5px) so it reads against candle bodies.
2. **Leader line** — thin (`rgba(255,255,255,0.38)`, 1px) from the dot to the pill.
3. **Pill** — rounded rect (rx=4, ~52×20px), fill = side color, text = the fill detail in mono font, centered. Buy pills use dark text (`#08231b`) on green; sell pills use white text on red.

Plus the existing **Avg Entry / Avg Exit dashed lines** (`stroke-dasharray 5 4`, 0.7 opacity) with their left-edge labels. These stay exactly as they are.

### 3b. The hard case (`ladder_decollision_prfx_5_on_one_bar.svg`)
PRFX trade 29: **five fills all on the 11:12 bar**, prices spanning 21 cents, including a **tight 1-cent pair** (5.00 and 5.01).
- All five **dots** sit at their true prices on the single bar (they stack, close together — that's fine and truthful).
- The five **pills** cannot stack at the same heights or they'd overlap into mush. So the de-collision sweep **fans them out vertically** with **angled** leader lines (dot stays at true price, pill slides up/down to a clear slot).
- The tight 1-cent pair is **forced apart by a minimum vertical gap** — even though their true prices are nearly identical, their pills get pushed to legible separation.
- Pills here are slightly wider (62px) to fit `10 @ 5.11`.

**This de-collision fan is the entire intellectual core of the feature.** Everything else is straightforward drawing.

### 3c. The aesthetic bar (both screenshots)
The chart must stay **clean**. The dots in Screenshot 2 are tasteful — they don't clutter. The ladder must not turn the chart into a wall of labels. This argues for: pills only where there's room, de-collision that respects clear space, and possibly a density cap (see §7 open questions).

---

## 4. The hard lesson from the prior attempt (why it froze, and the rule that follows)

The archived attempt recomputed the entire ladder geometry **every animation frame** via an `ISeriesPrimitive` whose `draw()` ran on every repaint. As the user panned/zoomed, the price scale auto-fit drifted (because indicator series were un-pinned), which moved the fill pixel positions every frame, which meant the "has it settled?" check never settled, which meant the repaint loop ran forever and froze the main thread.

We spent an entire session fixing symptoms (pin the scale, gate on autoScale, wake on dblclick, edge bounds) and each fix exposed another hole. The root realization:

> **The fills do not move. This is a journal snapshot, not a live trading terminal. The expensive geometry should be computed when the data or view changes — NOT 60 times per second.**

### The governing rule for this rebuild
**No per-frame recomputation. Ever.** The ladder layout is computed:
- once when the trade/fills load, and
- (only if the chart stays pannable) once more when the visible range *settles* after a gesture — debounced, not per-frame.

If we honor this rule, the freeze is **structurally impossible**, not merely patched.

---

## 5. The three-layer architecture

This separation is what makes the feature testable and freeze-proof. It also maps cleanly onto the future web port (per `ARCHITECTURE.md`): the brain is pure and portable, only the hands touch the chart library.

### Layer 1 — The Brain: pure layout (no chart, no React, no DOM)
A pure function. Signature, roughly:
```
computeFillLadder(
  fills: { price, qty, side, timeSec }[],
  view: { paneWidth, paneHeight, priceToY, timeToX, /* avg lines */ },
  opts: LadderOpts
) => LadderFrame   // { items: { dot, pill, leader }[], ... }
```
- Input: the fills + the chart's current pixel mappings (`priceToY`, `timeToX`) + dimensions.
- Output: for each fill, the dot position, the pill position (post-de-collision), and the leader line endpoints.
- **All de-collision lives here** — the vertical fan, the minimum-gap enforcement, the clear-space search, the side-of-bar choice.
- **Zero chart dependencies.** It takes pixel mappings as plain functions, so it can be unit-tested with fake mappings and asserted exactly (this is how we test the PRFX 1-cent-pair case deterministically).
- **This is the part the archive already solved well** — `fillLadderLayout.ts`, `buildOccupancy.ts`, `assembleLadderFrame.ts` on `wip/v0.2.4-freeze-setaside` are exactly this layer, with passing unit tests. The plumbing around them failed; *they* didn't. **Strong candidate to mine, not rebuild.**

### Layer 2 — The Hands: render (draw Layer 1's output)
Takes a `LadderFrame` and draws dots, leader lines, pills. Two implementation choices — see §6, the key decision.

### Layer 3 — The Nervous System: when to recompute (where the freeze lived)
The trigger policy. Options, cheapest first:
- **(A) Static chart:** compute once on load, paint once. The chart is locked (no pan/zoom). **Freeze is impossible by construction** — there is no second recompute, ever. Simplest possible Layer 3.
- **(B) Pannable, recompute-on-settle:** the chart stays interactive; the ladder recomputes only when the visible range *settles* (debounced after the gesture ends), never per-frame. More complex; this is the territory that bit us, though done correctly (debounced, not in `draw()`) it is safe.

**The §6 Decision 1 below picks A or B and cascades through everything.**

---

## 6. The three decisions that shape everything (Lao decides)

### Decision 1 — Static or pannable?
- **Static (recommended):** Frame the chart correctly on open (fit the day, pin the price range to the fills + a margin) and lock it — disable pan, zoom, axis-stretch, double-click-reset. Layer 3 becomes "compute once." Freeze impossible. Matches the product: this view is for *reading the execution*, not surfing.
- **Pannable:** Keep interaction; Layer 3 recomputes on settle. More moving parts; re-opens the class of bug we just escaped (but manageable if strictly debounced).

**Recommendation: Static.** It is dramatically simpler, it is the safe choice given the history, and it matches how a trader uses a post-trade review chart. Panning was never the point — the pinpoint info is.

> Counter-consideration: if you ever want users to zoom into a tight cluster to see detail, "static" forecloses that. But a better answer to that need is a density/zoom *control we own* (e.g. a "zoom to fills" button that re-frames and re-computes once), not free-form library panning. So even the zoom use-case doesn't require option B.

### Decision 2 — SVG overlay vs. canvas markers?
This is the real engineering tradeoff, and it has a concrete consequence we already discovered.

- **SVG overlay** (matches the design files literally; archived `FillLadderOverlay` was this):
  - Pros: pills, rounded rects, leader lines, fonts are trivial in SVG; matches the target exactly; easy to make crisp and themeable.
  - **Con (important): the branded screenshot export will NOT capture it.** The export screenshots the chart *canvas*; an SVG sibling floating over it is not part of that canvas. Confirmed in the prior session (Lao's branded export rendered the fill *lines* but not the overlay dots/pills). So SVG overlay = ladder is invisible in exported/shared images unless we also solve capture (e.g. composite the SVG onto the exported PNG ourselves).
- **Canvas markers / custom series primitive** (draw on the chart's own canvas):
  - Pros: screenshot export "just works" — it's all one canvas; the dots already shipped this way.
  - Cons: drawing rounded pills + text + leader lines on canvas is more code than SVG; text crispness/DPI handling is manual; and a *primitive* is what dragged us toward per-frame `draw()` last time (avoidable, but the temptation is there).

**This needs your call.** The screenshot capability is a real marketing asset (the Canva workflow). If shared images must show the ladder, canvas (or SVG-composited-into-export) wins. If the ladder is for *on-screen review only* and exports can stay simpler, SVG is faster to build and matches the SVGs.

> A third path: **SVG overlay for on-screen, plus an export compositor** that draws the same `LadderFrame` onto the exported PNG. Best of both, more work. Worth noting but probably not the first iteration.

### Decision 3 — Mine the archive or clean rebuild?
- **Mine (recommended for Layer 1):** lift the tested pure modules (`fillLadderLayout`, `buildOccupancy`, `assembleLadderFrame`) from `wip/v0.2.4-freeze-setaside`. They were never the bug — the per-frame plumbing was. Saves real time on the hardest part (de-collision).
- **Clean rebuild:** start the layout from scratch. Only worth it if we want a different de-collision approach than the archive's occupancy method.

**Recommendation: Mine Layer 1, rebuild Layers 2–3 fresh** (the plumbing is where the freeze lived, so that part starts clean). This keeps the good work and discards only the dangerous work.

## 7. Open questions to resolve during build (not blockers now)

1. **Density cap:** what happens with a 30-fill trade? Do we pill every fill, or pill the largest N and dot the rest? The clean-chart aesthetic (§3c) may demand a cap. (The Weekly-Review scalability lesson in the v0.2.0 plan rhymes here: lots of items need a summarization strategy.)
2. **Pill side choice:** always right of the bar? Or whichever side has clear space? The trade61 SVG places pills both left and right of dots.
3. **Avg lines interaction:** pills must not collide with the Avg Entry/Exit lines/labels either — de-collision should treat those as occupied space.
4. **Tiny trades:** 1–2 fills — probably just dot + pill, no fan needed. Confirm the trivial path is clean.
5. **Screenshot compositing** (only if Decision 2 = SVG): defer or build alongside?
6. **Fullscreen reflow:** even a static chart changes pane size on fullscreen toggle — Layer 1 must recompute once on pane-size change (this is a legitimate, bounded recompute, not per-frame).

## 8. Proposed build sequence (once decisions are locked)

Each step is a focused Claude Code session with diff-first review, per-file `git add`, typecheck+test gates, `Co-Authored-By: Claude Opus 4.8`, one concern per commit.

- **Step 0 — Mine & verify Layer 1.** Recover the pure layout modules from the archive branch onto the base, run their unit tests green in isolation. No chart wiring yet. Confirms the brain works on solid ground.
- **Step 1 — Render the simple case (Layer 2).** Draw dots + leader + pill for a 1–2 fill trade (GXAI id 61 baseline). Static. Confirm it looks like the SVG and the chart stays freeze-free.
- **Step 2 — De-collision on a cluster.** Wire the PRFX 5-on-one-bar case (id 29). Confirm the fan matches the SVG and the 1-cent pair separates.
- **Step 3 — Density + edge cases.** Big trades (CMND id 42, 9 fills), avg-line collision, fullscreen reflow.
- **Step 4 — Screenshot** (if Decision 2 requires it): make the ladder appear in branded exports.
- **Step 5 — Polish + ship ceremony.**

Test trades available: GXAI (id 61, baseline), PRFX (id 29, 5-on-one-bar stress), CMND (id 42, 9 fills), STI (id 66, the one we've been smoke-testing).

---

## 9. The non-negotiables (carried from the painful session)

1. **No per-frame recompute.** Layer 1 runs on load + bounded events (pane resize, optional settle). Never in a `draw()` that fires every frame.
2. **Pure layer stays pure.** No chart/electron/DOM imports in Layer 1 — keeps it testable and web-portable.
3. **Diff-first, gates, one concern per commit.** Every step reviewed as an actual diff, typecheck+test before commit, push as a separate beat.
4. **The chart stays clean.** If the ladder clutters, cap density. Aesthetics are a feature here, not an afterthought.
5. **If it starts to feel like we're fighting the chart again — stop.** That feeling was the early warning last time. The static approach should mean we never get there.

# ADDENDUM — Findings that REVISE the strategy above (2026-06-06, post-CMND forensics)

The sections above were written before we forensically read the archived branch and the
piece-1 era code. That investigation overturned two assumptions. **Where this addendum conflicts
with §1–§9 above, the addendum wins.** The earlier sections are kept for the reasoning trail.

## A. CORRECTION: how the CMND branded export actually got its ladder
Earlier we assumed the archive captured an SVG overlay into the PNG. **It did not.**
- The gorgeous CMND export (`fugaedge-CMND-2026-05-29.png`, full pills + dots + leaders baked in)
  was produced by the **piece-1 era code** (`4f7c938`, `fillLadderPrimitive.ts`), which drew the
  ladder **directly on the chart canvas** as an `IPrimitivePaneRenderer` with `zOrder: 'top'`.
  `takeScreenshot(true, false)` captures top-layer canvas primitives for free, so the ladder
  baked into the export automatically, then `composeBrandedScreenshot` wrapped it with the
  logo header and the Net P&L / Avg Entry / Avg Exit / Shares / Hold footer.
- The **archive tip** (`74531da`) renders the ladder as an **SVG sibling overlay**
  (`FillLadderOverlay`, `absolute inset-0`). Its export is still `takeScreenshot()` (canvas only)
  with **no DOM/SVG capture and no compositing** — so the archive-tip branded export would show
  **NO ladder at all.**

**Consequence:** the SVG-overlay approach is fundamentally incompatible with the current
canvas-only export. Lao's requirement (ladder MUST appear in branded exports — it's a core
build-in-public asset) therefore **rules out the SVG-overlay-only path** unless we also build an
export-time compositor. The export-friendly approach is **render the ladder ON the canvas.**

## B. THE BIG FINDING: the freeze was never canvas-vs-SVG — it was the price scale
History on the archive (`863b21c` note): the **piece-1 canvas primitive ALSO froze**, which is
*why* the team switched to the SVG overlay (piece-2). Then the **SVG overlay also froze** (the
freeze we fought all session). CC connected them:

> Both froze for the **same root cause**: the **unpinned price scale drifted** (indicator series
> on the right scale were not pinned), which forced lightweight-charts to **re-render every
> frame**, which (a) called the canvas primitive's `draw()` every frame → piece-1 freeze, and
> (b) fired the SVG repaint loop every frame → piece-2 freeze. The renderer was a red herring.
> The bug lived in the scale.

The team changed rendering technology **twice** trying to outrun a bug that was in the scale the
whole time. We independently proved tonight that pinning the scale (scale-level
`setVisibleRange` + `setAutoScale(false)`) stops the per-frame re-render.

## C. REVISED ARCHITECTURE (supersedes §5 Layer-2 and §6 Decision-2)
**Render the ladder as a canvas primitive (piece-1 mechanism) + pin the price scale.**
1. **Canvas primitive render** (`IPrimitivePaneRenderer`, `zOrder: 'top'`): draws dot + leader +
   `QTY @ PRICE` pill on the chart's own canvas → **captured into branded exports for free**,
   exactly like the CMND PNG. This is the export-friendly path, confirmed by evidence.
2. **Reset-proof price-scale pin** (recoverable insight from tonight, even though tonight's code
   is set aside): pin the right scale to the day range with `setAutoScale(false)`, re-assert it
   if a double-click reset flips autoScale back on. With the scale pinned, the chart does NOT
   re-render every frame → the primitive's `draw()` is NOT called every frame → **no
   per-frame-canvas-draw freeze.** This is what makes the canvas primitive safe THIS time when it
   wasn't before.
3. **Mine the pure layout brain** from the archive (unchanged, tested, never the bug): both
   generations exist and are recoverable —
   - piece-1: simpler `layoutFillLadder(pillOffsetX)`,
   - piece-2: free-space `layoutFillLadder` + `buildOccupancy` + `assembleLadderFrame`.
   Pick one as the de-collision engine (piece-2's free-space version is the richer one that
   produced the fan in the PRFX SVG).

## D. REVISED Decision 1 (static vs pannable) — now a PRODUCT choice, not a safety one
Earlier (§6) leaned "static" partly to dodge the freeze. **That reason is gone:** the scale-pin
kills the freeze whether or not the chart pans. So static-vs-pannable is now a pure product/UX
preference:
- **Static / framed-on-open:** simplest Layer 3 (compute once, on load + on pane resize), and a
  clean fit for a post-trade review chart whose job is showing the execution. Still recommended
  for simplicity, but no longer *required* for safety.
- **Pannable:** now safe with the pin, but Layer 3 must recompute the ladder geometry on
  visible-range *settle* (debounced, never per-frame), and the pin must hold across pan + reset.
DECISION DEFERRED by Lao — not blocking Step 0.

## E. WHAT THIS MEANS FOR THE BUILD SEQUENCE
- **§8 Step 4 ("Screenshot — if Decision 2 requires it") is no longer a separate late step.**
  Because the ladder is a canvas primitive, export capture is automatic — there is nothing extra
  to build for the PNG (beyond confirming `takeScreenshot(true,…)` + `composeBrandedScreenshot`
  still capture it, which the CMND PNG proves they do).
- **A new early step is required: the price-scale pin.** The canvas primitive is only safe once
  the scale is pinned. So the pin is not optional polish — it is a prerequisite that must land
  with (or before) the primitive. Sequence becomes:
  - **Step 0 — Mine & verify the pure layout brain** (unchanged; safe; identical regardless of
    other decisions).
  - **Step 0.5 — Land the reset-proof price-scale pin on the base** and verify (with the blob-dot
    chart we have now) that pan/zoom/reset stay freeze-free. This is the foundation the primitive
    sits on, and it's verifiable on its own before any ladder code.
  - **Step 1+ — Canvas-primitive ladder** (simple case → de-collision → density), each on the
    pinned-scale foundation.

  per-frame *re-render* that would call the primitive's draw() every frame.)
